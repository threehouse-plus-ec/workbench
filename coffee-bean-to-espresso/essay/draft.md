# From raw coffee beans to espresso — stir or not stir?

## Opening

A single espresso shot is the endpoint of a chain that stretches from altitude and soil through fermentation, drying, roasting chemistry, grinding physics, and pressurised water flow. At nearly every stage, someone has a strong opinion about what matters most. The stirring question — whether to redistribute grounds before tamping — sits at the final link of the chain, but it reveals the tension between craft intuition and measurable extraction science that runs through the entire journey.

This essay traces the path from cherry to cup, pausing at each stage to ask: what is engineering, what is chemistry, and what is ritual? The Breakwater entries in this topic folder analyse specific claims; the essay synthesises.

## Synthesis

- The journey as a sequence of phase transitions: green → roasted (Maillard, caramelisation, pyrolysis), roasted → ground (fracture mechanics, bimodal particle distribution), ground → puck (arrangement, compaction), puck → liquid (percolation, dissolution, emulsion).
- Each transition has controllable parameters and uncontrollable ones. The stirring question matters precisely because it sits at a transition where the barista still has agency.
- The deeper question: does the specialty coffee community's emphasis on preparation ritual (WDT tools, precision scales, flow profiling) reflect genuine sensitivity to extraction physics, or does it function as a craft identity marker — a way of signalling expertise through visible procedure?

## Open questions

- How does degassing (CO₂ release post-roast) interact with puck distribution? A perfectly distributed dry bed may behave differently from the same bed 48 hours later.
- Is there a grind quality threshold below which stirring cannot compensate for poor particle uniformity?
- The social dimension: how did pour-over and espresso cultures diverge in their relationship to procedural rigour?
