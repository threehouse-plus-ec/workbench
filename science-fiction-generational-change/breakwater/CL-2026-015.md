# CL-2026-015 — Golden Age SF readership became the Apollo engineering generation

**Selection mode:** SWEEP  
**Domain:** Sociology of technology, generational influence  
**Scope:** American science fiction readership 1938–1955; NASA/contractor engineering workforce 1960–1972

---

## Claim

The generation that grew up reading Golden Age science fiction (Asimov, Clarke, Heinlein; roughly the Astounding Science Fiction cohort of 1938–1955) disproportionately entered aerospace engineering and formed the core technical workforce of the Apollo programme, with their early science fiction reading functioning as a formative motivational influence.

## Predictions

If true: (1) biographical and survey data from Apollo-era engineers should show higher science fiction readership than age-matched controls in other engineering fields; (2) recruitment and career-choice narratives from this cohort should cite science fiction as a motivating factor at rates above chance; (3) the effect should be specific to space-related careers, not a general correlation between reading fiction and entering engineering.

## Constraints

- Selection bias: engineers who later became prominent are more likely to be interviewed and to construct coherent origin narratives.
- The post-Sputnik funding surge (1957–) was an independent causal factor driving aerospace career entry regardless of childhood reading.
- Science fiction readership and engineering aptitude may share a common cause (e.g. curiosity, mathematical inclination) without the former causing the latter.
- The claim is about a generation, not individuals — statistical evidence is needed, not anecdotes.
