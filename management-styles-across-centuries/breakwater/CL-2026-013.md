# CL-2026-013 — Hierarchical command was optimal for pre-telegraph trading companies

**Selection mode:** SWEEP  
**Domain:** Organisational history, communication infrastructure  
**Scope:** European chartered trading companies, 1600–1850

---

## Claim

The rigid hierarchical command structures of the East India Company (EIC), VOC, and similar chartered companies were not merely cultural preferences but were functionally optimal given communication latencies of 6–18 months between headquarters and operations.

## Predictions

If true: (1) decision-making authority should have been delegated to on-site managers for time-sensitive matters even within the hierarchical structure (i.e. the hierarchy was nominal, not operational, at the frontier); (2) companies that attempted tighter central control should have performed worse in fast-changing theatres; (3) as communication latency decreased (telegraph, 1850s onwards), organisational structures should have centralised rather than remaining delegated.

## Constraints

- "Optimal" is relative to the company's objectives, which included monopoly rent extraction, not just efficiency.
- The EIC was simultaneously a commercial enterprise and an instrument of state — its structure served political as well as economic functions.
- Documentation bias: we know more about the EIC and VOC than about smaller competitors that may have used different structures.
