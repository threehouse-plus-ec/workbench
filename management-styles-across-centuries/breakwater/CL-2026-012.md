# CL-2026-012 — Toyota Production System caused Toyota's competitive advantage

**Selection mode:** REQUEST  
**Domain:** Management history, industrial organisation  
**Scope:** Toyota Motor Corporation, 1950–1990

---

## Claim

The Toyota Production System (TPS) — lean manufacturing, just-in-time, kaizen, jidoka — was the primary cause of Toyota's competitive advantage over American and European automakers in the second half of the twentieth century.

## Predictions

If true: (1) Toyota's quality and cost metrics should have improved specifically after TPS implementation, not before; (2) other companies adopting TPS principles should show comparable improvements; (3) Toyota's advantage should be explainable primarily through TPS practices rather than through external factors such as exchange rates, labour costs, trade policy, or keiretsu supplier relationships.

## Constraints

- Post-war Japanese industrial policy (MITI guidance, protected home market) is an independent variable.
- Yen-dollar exchange rate movements affected relative competitiveness independently of production method.
- Survivorship bias: TPS is studied because Toyota succeeded; failed Japanese manufacturers using similar methods receive less attention.
- Causal attribution in complex systems is inherently difficult — multiple sufficient causes may coexist.
