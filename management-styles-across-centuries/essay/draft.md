# Management styles across centuries — co-evolution with infrastructure

## Opening

Every celebrated management style is entangled with the infrastructure that made it possible. The question is whether management innovation drives organisational success, or whether favourable environments produce successful organisations that then narrate their practices as causal. This essay examines the co-evolution of management philosophy and communication, transport, and information technology across four centuries — not to debunk management thinking, but to locate it within the material conditions that constrained and enabled it.

## Synthesis

**Phase 1 — Sail and ledger (1600–1850).** The chartered trading companies operated under extreme information asymmetry. A letter from London to Madras took six months. The management "style" was not chosen from a menu — it was the only structure that could function when the principal could not observe the agent. Delegation was not empowerment; it was necessity.

**Phase 2 — Wire and rail (1850–1950).** The telegraph collapsed communication latency from months to hours. Frederick Taylor's scientific management became conceivable only when a factory manager could receive instructions the same day. The assembly line required synchronised timing that pre-telegraph organisations could not have coordinated. Management "innovation" tracked infrastructure, not the reverse.

**Phase 3 — Container and chip (1950–2000).** Containerisation collapsed logistics costs; the microprocessor collapsed information-processing costs. Toyota's lean production exploited both — just-in-time requires reliable, fast, cheap logistics and precise inventory tracking. Could TPS have worked in 1920? The principles perhaps; the execution, certainly not.

**Phase 4 — Network and algorithm (2000–present).** Flat organisations, remote work, agile methodology — all presuppose cheap, instantaneous, global communication. The "management style" of a distributed tech company is not a philosophical choice; it is the natural topology of a system connected by near-zero-latency links.

**The pattern:** Each era's dominant management style is not best practice discovered by genius — it is the organisational form that the prevailing infrastructure makes locally optimal. When the infrastructure shifts, the management style follows, usually with a lag during which the old form is defended as principle rather than recognised as contingency.

## Open questions

- Where does culture enter? Japanese and American firms operated in the same post-war infrastructure but developed different management philosophies. Is this a counterexample to the infrastructure-determinism thesis, or does culture function as a slower-moving infrastructure?
- How should we treat management styles that were imposed (colonialism, command economies) versus those that emerged from competitive selection?
- The current moment: does algorithmic management (gig economy, surveillance metrics) represent a new phase, or is it Phase 2 (Taylorism) replayed with better sensors?

## Constraints acknowledged

This essay treats management as organisational practice, not as individual leadership psychology. It does not cover leadership charisma, founder mythology, or the self-help industry that has grown around management discourse.
