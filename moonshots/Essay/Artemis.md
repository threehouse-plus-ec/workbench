# Return to the Moon: NASA’s Artemis Program and Its First Crewed Flight

**A Review Article — 6 April 2026 (Rev 4)**

*This article examines the scope, technical details, costs, and open questions of NASA’s effort to send humans back to the Moon — and the first crewed mission now under way. All mission-status claims reflect publicly reported information as of 6 April 2026. The mission’s final test — returning the crew safely through Earth’s atmosphere — has not yet occurred.*

-----

## 1. Where Artemis Came From

NASA’s Artemis program did not start from scratch. It grew out of hardware, contracts, and political commitments stretching back more than fifteen years.

In 2005, after the Space Shuttle *Columbia* disaster, the United States committed to a new programme called Constellation, which aimed to build a heavy rocket and crew capsule for flights beyond low Earth orbit. Constellation was cancelled in 2010 — already over budget and behind schedule — but its main components survived. Congress directed NASA to build a new heavy rocket called the Space Launch System, using the Shuttle’s main engines and solid-fuel boosters. The Orion crew capsule, originally designed for Constellation, continued development under a new name and new mission goals.

One critical piece of the spacecraft comes from Europe. The European Space Agency builds the service module — the unpiloted section behind the crew cabin that provides power, propulsion, water, and air. This arrangement was agreed in 2013, when NASA was facing deep budget cuts. Rather than building its own service module, NASA accepted ESA’s contribution, saving the agency billions of dollars. In return, ESA counted the work toward its financial obligations to the International Space Station.

The result is a programme assembled from legacy parts and international deals, not designed from a blank page. That history matters, because many of the cost and schedule problems described later in this article are rooted in decisions made long before the Artemis name existed.

### The current plan

Artemis is structured as a series of increasingly ambitious missions:

Artemis I (November 2022) sent an uncrewed Orion capsule around the Moon and back, testing the rocket and spacecraft for the first time.

Artemis II (launched 1 April 2026) is the subject of this article: the first crewed flight, sending four astronauts on a loop around the Moon without landing.

Artemis III (planned for mid-2027) will test commercially built lunar landers in Earth orbit, rather than attempting a landing — a role change announced in early 2026.

Artemis IV (targeted for early 2028) is now planned as the first crewed landing on the Moon since 1972.

Artemis V (targeted for late 2028) would be the second landing, with initial steps toward building a permanent base.

International partnerships are governed by the Artemis Accords, a set of principles for peaceful lunar exploration now signed by more than 60 countries.

### Two big changes in 2026

NASA’s new administrator, Jared Isaacman — a former commercial astronaut confirmed by the Senate in December 2025 — made two significant structural decisions shortly after taking office.

First, in February 2026, NASA announced it would halt work on the planned upgrade to the rocket’s upper stage — a more powerful version known as Block 1B, built around a new Exploration Upper Stage — and move toward a standardised upper-stage approach, locking the Space Launch System into its current configuration for the foreseeable future.

Second, on 24 March 2026, Isaacman announced that the Lunar Gateway — a small space station that was supposed to orbit the Moon and serve as a transfer point for landing missions — would be “paused in its current form.” NASA said it would redirect those resources toward building a base directly on the lunar surface, at an estimated cost of $20 billion over seven years. Agency officials stressed that Gateway is being paused rather than formally terminated, and that some of its already-built hardware may be repurposed. In operational terms, however, Gateway has dropped out of near-term Artemis planning, even if NASA has stopped short of formal termination.

What was lost? Gateway would have provided a staging point in lunar orbit — a place where crews could transfer to landing vehicles, where landers could be reused between missions, and where supplies could be stored independently of crew transport schedules. Without it, each landing mission must be more self-contained, which reduces operational flexibility but simplifies the architecture.

## 2. Artemis II: What the Mission Is Doing

### The crew

Four astronauts are flying aboard the Orion spacecraft, which the crew named *Integrity*:

Reid Wiseman (50), Commander — a NASA astronaut and former International Space Station commander who is leading the mission. A test pilot by training, he brings deep experience in complex flight operations.

Victor Glover (49), Pilot — a U.S. Navy aviator and veteran of a previous SpaceX mission to the Space Station. He is the first Black astronaut assigned to a lunar mission.

Christina Koch (47), Mission Specialist — a NASA astronaut who holds the record for the longest single spaceflight by a woman (328 days on the Space Station). She has conducted multiple spacewalks and brings extensive scientific expertise.

Jeremy Hansen (50), Mission Specialist — a Royal Canadian Air Force colonel and Canadian Space Agency astronaut. He is the first Canadian to travel to the Moon’s vicinity. Hansen proposed the spacecraft’s name, *Integrity*, to reflect both the mission’s technical purpose (proving the spacecraft works) and its aspirational meaning.

Canada’s role in Artemis extends beyond having an astronaut on board. The Canadian Space Agency has decades of experience building robotic arms for the Shuttle and Space Station, and its Lunar Utility Vehicle is planned as a Canadian contribution to the future Moon base.

### The trajectory

The mission follows what engineers call a “free-return trajectory.” Here is what that means in plain terms: after launching from Florida and orbiting Earth twice to check systems, the spacecraft fired its engine to head toward the Moon. The path is designed so that the Moon’s gravity swings the spacecraft around and sends it back toward Earth — even if no major propulsion burn were available after the initial push toward the Moon. It is like rolling a ball along a curved track: once set in motion, the shape of the track brings it back. This is a safety feature. If something goes wrong with the engine, the crew still comes home.

That said, the spacecraft is not entirely passive. It still needs small thruster firings to control its orientation and to aim precisely at the narrow corridor of atmosphere it must hit during reentry. The free-return trajectory provides a safety net, not autopilot.

On 6 April 2026, the spacecraft swung around the far side of the Moon, passing within roughly 4,070 miles (6,550 kilometres) of the surface. At its farthest point, the crew reached approximately 252,760 miles (406,773 kilometres) from Earth — about 4,100 miles farther than any human has ever been, surpassing the record held by the Apollo 13 crew since 1970.

The total distance from launch to splashdown is projected at roughly 695,000 miles. The crew is expected to splash down off the coast of San Diego around 10 April, where a U.S. Navy vessel, the USS *John P. Murtha*, will recover them.

### What the crew is testing

Artemis II is fundamentally a test flight. The crew’s job is to put the spacecraft through its paces in deep space — conditions that cannot be fully simulated on the ground. They are evaluating life-support systems (air, water, temperature), navigation and communication over vast distances, propulsion, power, and the ability to manually fly the spacecraft. They are also conducting scientific observations of the Moon and collecting data on how the human body responds to the deep-space radiation environment.

This is not a routine trip. It is the first time humans have ridden this rocket and this spacecraft together. The only previous flight of the combined system was the uncrewed Artemis I mission in 2022. And the most demanding phase — returning through Earth’s atmosphere — has not yet happened.

### Why reentry is the hardest part

When Orion returns from the Moon, it will hit Earth’s atmosphere at roughly 25,000 miles per hour (40,000 kilometres per hour). At that speed, the air in front of the capsule compresses so violently that temperatures on the spacecraft’s exterior exceed 5,000 degrees Fahrenheit (2,760 degrees Celsius) — hot enough to melt steel. A protective shield on the bottom of the capsule absorbs this heat by slowly burning away, carrying the energy with it rather than letting it reach the crew inside.

To put this in perspective: when astronauts return from the International Space Station, they are travelling at roughly 17,500 miles per hour. Artemis II will be moving about 40 per cent faster. Because energy increases with the square of speed, the reentry from lunar distance is roughly twice as energetic as a return from low Earth orbit. No crewed vehicle has faced these conditions in over fifty years, and, as the next section explains, the heat shield has been a source of significant concern.

## 3. The Heat-Shield Problem

### What went wrong on the previous flight

During Artemis I — the uncrewed test flight in 2022 — the heat shield did not behave as expected. After the capsule splashed down in the Pacific Ocean, inspectors found more than 100 spots where chunks of the protective material had broken off, leaving gouges and holes in the shield’s surface.

The shield is made of a material called AVCOAT — a substance originally developed during the Apollo era. It is designed to burn away in a controlled, gradual fashion during reentry, like a candle melting: the material chars and flakes off smoothly, carrying heat away from the spacecraft. What happened on Artemis I was different. Instead of eroding evenly, pieces broke off in irregular chunks — not how the material is supposed to work.

NASA initially described the issue as minor. Detailed photographs of the damage were not released publicly until May 2024, when NASA’s internal watchdog — the Office of Inspector General — included them in a report. By that point, the full extent of the problem was clear.

### Why it happened: a pressure problem inside the shield

After more than 100 laboratory tests at facilities across the country, NASA identified the cause. The explanation involves a kind of internal pressure build-up, which is easier to understand with an analogy.

Imagine baking bread. As the dough heats up, gases form inside it. If the dough is porous enough, those gases escape gradually, and the bread rises evenly. But if the outer crust hardens too quickly — sealing in the gases before they can escape — pressure builds up inside, and eventually the crust cracks open.

Something similar happened inside the heat shield. During reentry, Orion used a technique called “skip entry”: the capsule dipped into the upper atmosphere, slowed down, then briefly popped back above the atmosphere before dipping in again for final descent — like a stone skipping on water. This technique spreads the heating over a longer period and allows the capsule to target a precise landing spot.

The problem was in the “skip” phase. When the capsule rose back above the atmosphere between dips, the external heating rate dropped sharply. But inside the shield material, residual heat continued to generate gases through a chemical process called pyrolysis (the same process that turns wood into charcoal). Normally, those gases would seep outward through the porous char layer and escape harmlessly. But the AVCOAT on Artemis I was not porous enough. The gases became trapped, pressure built up inside the material, and the outer layer cracked and broke off in chunks.

The porosity problem was made worse by a change in how the shield was manufactured. During an earlier test flight in 2014, workers had filled 300,000 tiny honeycomb cells in the shield by hand, one by one, with the protective material. For Artemis I and II, the manufacturer switched to a faster method: pre-making 186 larger blocks and bonding them onto the shield’s surface. This saved significant production time, but the resulting material turned out to be less porous — partly because lower porosity made it easier to inspect with ultrasonic testing equipment. In an ironic twist, a property chosen to make quality control easier created the conditions for the failure.

### What NASA decided to do about it

Replacing the heat shield was not an option. The shield for Artemis II was already bonded to the capsule before Artemis I even flew. As one former NASA engineer put it, there was no way to simply remove and replace it.

Instead, NASA convened an independent review panel in April 2024, led by Paul Hill, a veteran flight director who had led the Space Shuttle’s return-to-flight effort after the *Columbia* disaster. The panel completed its work in December 2024. However, the publicly released version of its findings was heavily edited, with substantial sections blacked out. This drew criticism from former NASA engineers and astronauts who questioned whether the level of disclosure was adequate for a decision involving crew safety.

NASA’s solution was to change the flight plan rather than the hardware. For Artemis II, the reentry path has been modified to reduce the time the capsule spends in the conditions that caused the damage. NASA describes this as a steeper descent angle with reduced exposure to peak heating — effectively minimising the “skip” that triggered the gas-trapping problem on Artemis I. The crew trained for this revised profile, which differs from the original plans developed earlier in the mission’s preparation.

### The other side of the argument

It is important to present the engineering counterargument fairly. Heat shields are inherently unpredictable systems. Even during the Apollo era, AVCOAT showed variation in how it charred and eroded from one flight to the next. No two reentries are exactly alike in their physical chemistry. The skip-entry technique itself is a safety measure — it spreads heating loads over a longer path, reducing peak temperatures compared to a straight plunge into the atmosphere.

NASA’s decision can be understood not as ignoring a known defect but as accepting a bounded risk based on extensive testing. The agency conducted arc-jet tests (laboratory simulations using superheated gas streams), wind-tunnel campaigns, and material-sample analysis to validate the modified flight path. Its conclusion was that the changed reentry conditions bring the shield’s performance back within safe limits.

NASA has also announced that future heat shields — starting with Artemis III — will be manufactured with improved porosity to prevent the gas-trapping problem from recurring. This does not necessarily mean the current shield is unsafe; it may simply reflect the normal process of refining a manufacturing method based on what the first flights reveal.

The irreducible uncertainty, however, is this: no ground test can perfectly replicate what happens to a full-size heat shield during an actual return from the Moon at 25,000 miles per hour. The definitive answer will come only when Orion re-enters Earth’s atmosphere at the end of this mission — an event still days away as this article is written.

## 4. What It Costs and Why It Keeps Getting More Expensive

### The headline numbers

A 2021 audit by NASA’s Office of Inspector General — the agency’s independent financial watchdog — estimated that each launch of the Space Launch System rocket and Orion capsule costs approximately $4.1 billion. That figure covers launch operations for at least the first four Artemis missions. It does not include the full cost of developing the rocket in the first place.

The same audit projected total Artemis programme spending from 2012 through 2025 at roughly $93 billion. That figure covers not just the rocket and capsule but also launch facilities, the (now-paused) Gateway station, commercially contracted lunar landers, spacesuits, and associated support.

To put these numbers in proportion: NASA’s total budget for 2026 is about $24.4 billion. The Space Launch System alone accounts for roughly $4.1 billion of that — about 17 per cent of everything NASA does. Over the programme’s lifetime, Artemis has consumed roughly $6 to $7 billion per year since 2017.

### How it compares to Apollo

The Apollo programme — which put twelve men on the Moon between 1969 and 1972 — cost approximately $290 billion in today’s money, with annual spending peaking at the equivalent of about $42 billion per year. Artemis is projected to spend roughly $105 billion through its first crewed landing (now expected no earlier than 2028).

On paper, Artemis looks cheaper overall. But the per-flight comparison is less favourable. Each Space Launch System launch costs roughly $4 billion, compared to an estimated $1.4 billion per Saturn V launch in today’s dollars. And unlike Apollo, which benefited from a massive surge of national funding during the space race with the Soviet Union, Artemis must compete for resources within a constrained federal budget.

### Why costs keep growing

The original projection when the Space Launch System programme began was roughly $500 million per launch. The White House’s own 2026 budget proposal described the rocket as “grossly expensive” and noted that costs had exceeded the original budget by 140 per cent.

Several factors explain the escalation. First, the rocket was originally supposed to fly by 2016. Its first launch came six years late, in November 2022. Every month of delay means continued spending on workforce, facilities, and stored hardware. The programme slipped its first launch date more than 26 times during development.

Second, contractor oversight has been a persistent weakness. Boeing, which builds the rocket’s main stage, received nearly 90 per cent of its available performance bonuses between 2013 and 2017 — a period of deepening delays and rising costs. A 2018 watchdog report found poor expenditure tracking and contract extensions granted without adequate oversight.

Third, the Space Launch System is not reusable. Unlike SpaceX’s rockets, which land and fly again, each Space Launch System is used once and discarded. That means NASA must rebuild the entire rocket for every mission. Combined with a very slow launch rate — only two flights in roughly four years — the programme has had few opportunities to learn from experience and improve efficiency.

Despite all of this, Congress has consistently protected the programme’s funding. A proposed 24 per cent cut to NASA’s budget was rejected in January 2026. The political durability of the Space Launch System — sometimes called the “Senate Launch System” by critics — reflects the fact that its construction supports jobs in key states and congressional districts. Whether Administrator Isaacman’s recent structural decisions will break this pattern or merely shift spending from one programme to another remains to be seen.

## 5. The Bigger Questions

### Is the Space Launch System the right rocket?

Much of the public debate treats this as a simple question: the Space Launch System is expensive, commercial rockets are cheaper, so why not switch? The reality is more nuanced.

The Space Launch System is a deliberately conservative design. Its engines and boosters have decades of flight history from the Space Shuttle era. Its safety-certification process follows well-established government standards. NASA controls the programme and certification chain more directly than it would under a commercial-services model. These are not trivial advantages when human lives are at stake. The argument for the Space Launch System is not that it is efficient but that it is *known* — its failure modes are understood, its components are tested, and its regulatory framework is mature.

The alternative — relying on commercially built rockets for deep-space crew transport — introduces a different kind of risk. SpaceX’s Starship, the leading commercial candidate for future lunar missions, is designed around rapid iteration, reusability, and dramatically lower cost per kilogram. But its plan for landing on the Moon requires a chain of roughly 12 separate refuelling flights in Earth orbit before each crewed mission. Each flight in that chain must succeed for the lunar landing to proceed. A single failure in the refuelling sequence — a docking malfunction, a booster problem, a scheduling delay — could postpone an entire Moon mission without anything being wrong with the lander itself. NASA’s own safety advisory panel has flagged the development of Starship’s next-generation engines as a critical risk to the programme’s timeline.

Blue Origin, the other company contracted to build a lunar lander, has not yet flown its prototype vehicle.

In short, the Space Launch System carries the risk of economic unsustainability — it costs too much to fly often enough. Commercial alternatives carry the risk of systemic complexity — too many things must go right in sequence. Neither risk is obviously worse than the other, and a well-managed programme would ideally find ways to retire both. The current political and budgetary environment does not offer easy solutions.

### Is NASA being transparent enough?

This question requires separating NASA’s official position from the concerns raised by independent observers.

NASA’s position is straightforward: the agency conducted extensive analysis — more than 100 tests at specialised facilities — confirmed the root cause of the heat-shield damage, validated a modified flight path, and concluded that the mission meets all safety standards. Administrator Isaacman acknowledged in January 2026 that spaceflight always involves uncertainty, but affirmed that the agency’s engineering process provides sufficient confidence.

The concerns are equally concrete. The independent review panel’s report on the heat shield was released with substantial sections blacked out, and NASA has not publicly explained why. Detailed photographs of the Artemis I heat-shield damage were not made available until roughly 17 months after the capsule returned — and they were published by the watchdog office, not by the programme itself. NASA has also resisted establishing clear, mission-by-mission cost figures for the Space Launch System, making it difficult for outside analysts to assess the programme’s fiscal trajectory independently.

Several observers have pointed out an apparent inconsistency: if a commercially operated spacecraft — like those built by SpaceX or Boeing for trips to the Space Station — returned from orbit with similar heat-shield damage, NASA would almost certainly require a redesign and an uncrewed test flight to prove the fix worked. The agency has not applied that standard to its own flagship programme.

This pattern echoes dynamics documented after the *Challenger* (1986) and *Columbia* (2003) shuttle disasters, where investigations found that institutional pressures — schedule deadlines, cost concerns, organisational culture — had created gaps between what engineers knew and what decision-makers communicated. It is important to be clear: nothing comparable to those acute safety failures has been demonstrated in the Artemis case. The heat-shield issue is a materials-performance problem with a documented cause and an engineering response. The concern is not that Artemis is repeating those disasters, but whether the same kinds of institutional pressures — schedule momentum, political visibility, sunk costs — could be quietly shaping how risks are communicated.

### Where things stand right now

As this article is written, four people are farther from Earth than any human has been in more than fifty years. The Orion spacecraft *Integrity* has demonstrated that it can sustain its crew in deep space, communicate over vast distances, and navigate the gravitational landscape between Earth and Moon. Commander Wiseman described watching the entire globe from pole to pole — Africa, Europe, the northern lights visible simultaneously — as “the most spectacular moment” of the flight, one that “paused all four of us in our tracks.”

On 6 April 2026, the crew surpassed the all-time human distance record, travelling farther from Earth than the Apollo 13 astronauts did in 1970.

But the mission’s most important test is still ahead. The heat shield — the subject of years of investigation, debate, and a redesigned flight plan — will face its definitive trial when Orion plunges back into Earth’s atmosphere at 25,000 miles per hour. That moment, expected around 10 April, will determine not just whether this crew comes home safely, but whether the entire engineering approach that NASA has staked the programme on is sound.

The larger question is not whether Artemis can send people near the Moon. It has now done that. The question is whether the infrastructure being built — the rocket, the capsule, the landers, the base — can be sustained at a cost and pace that justify the investment before political patience or public funding runs out. That is a question only the missions that follow Artemis II can answer.
